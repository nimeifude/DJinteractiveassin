Winter is coming —— Where do Boston’s homeless go after the COVID-19 pandemic?

by lingxiao Liu and Jiazhen Zhang, October 24, 2023.

The rehousing efforts for the homeless have stalled as shelters have had to reduce their capacity by half in order to mitigate the spread of the virus as a result of the COVID-19 pandemic. Therefore the number of encampments multiplied, especially represented by the stubborn Mass&Cass, which became the first part of Boston to be dealt with.

Homeless people and their situation are often misunderstood and overlooked, and we can start by looking at the factors that contribute to being homeless, learning about the distribution of homeless shelters in Boston, and helping Boston's homeless population to weather the storm by learning about the organizations, agencies, and programs that serve homeless people who wish to seek shelter and improve their situation.

The pandemic magnified the important tie between housing and health care, and the Boston Public Health Commission responded quickly to try to achieve success through innovative practices in collaboration, outreach, shelter, supportive services, and housing.

7 Criteria for Determining Risk of Homelessness

Amelia Najjar, a student at Tufts University, investigated and summarized the distribution of homeless shelters in Boston and the resources available to them. Homeless people were categorized and researched separately based on seven characteristics, which were combined with data on walking distances between shelters and the nearest services in different areas, resulting in seven maps of areas at risk of homelessness.

Using census tracts, a map was created for each characteristic. For a given characteristic, each tract was ranked from one to four, with one being the lowest concentration and four being the highest concentration of the variable. For each tract She summed the rankings for all seven variables to yield an overall ranking. The tracts with the highest ranking were the most at-risk of being homeless.

Homeless, unhoused, or housing-deprived?

When first used in the United States in the 1870s, the term “homelessness” was meant to describe itinerant “tramps” traversing the country in search of work. Today, homelessness is primarily a condition of a lack of stable, safe, and functional housing. While crime, drugs, and laziness account for only a small proportion of the cases in which people lose their homes, the causes of homelessness for the majority of people are complex, painful, and difficult to change. They are not incorrigible, but their social situation makes it much more difficult for them to leave their current state. “What usually happens is that, over time, a term that may have been neutral at some point becomes pejorative,” said Robin Queen, a linguistics professor at the University of Michigan. We have to recognize that the neutral word "homeless" has now acquired discriminatory, ugly hidden meanings that are unfair to those struggling to change their circumstances.

Until we know a person's story, it might be more prudent to use more neutral words, such as unhoused or housing-deprived.


But at the end of the day, the root cause of homelessness is housing, and Boston, which has the smallest percentage of homelessness of any major city, has done a lot of hard work in the post-COVID-19 era and with all the nonprofits working to address the situation of homeless people, and has seen promising results.
